About This Tracker

Research Question

This tracker monitors legislation across all 50 U.S. states and the U.S. federal government to answer a specific question: does a given bill materially affect what a user sees in their algorithmic feed?

That means we are not simply tracking any bill that mentions social media or artificial intelligence. We are specifically looking for legislation that would change the mechanics of how content is selected, ranked, or presented to users — through user controls, platform design requirements, transparency mandates, or restrictions on algorithmic personalization.

How Bills Are Collected

Bills are pulled from LegiScan, a national legislative data service that aggregates bill information from all 50 state legislatures and Congress. We search using 23 keyword combinations — including terms like "algorithmic transparency," "social media recommendation," "user control algorithm," and "automated decision" — across every active legislative session.

Every bill returned by those searches is run through our relevance scoring system. Bills that score above a minimum threshold are stored; the rest are discarded. We err on the side of collecting more rather than less, since the cost of missing a relevant bill is higher than the cost of reviewing an irrelevant one.

The tracker re-runs these searches periodically. When a bill has not changed since the last run, we skip re-fetching its full details to avoid redundant work.

How Bills Are Scored

Every bill goes through an automated relevance scoring system that produces a score from 0 to 10, which maps to one of five tiers:

DIRECT Bill directly addresses algorithmic feed control — for example, requiring platforms to offer a chronological feed option, mandating user opt-out of personalized recommendations, or restricting ranking signals for minors.
HIGH Bill substantially regulates algorithmic curation, including strong default settings for minors or significant platform design requirements tied to feed behavior.
MEDIUM Bill meaningfully affects feed content through parental controls, age-gating, or platform design requirements that would change what users see, even if not the primary focus.
LOW Bill has indirect or marginal feed impact — for example, data privacy requirements that may limit personalization, or generic transparency mandates without user-facing controls.
MINIMAL Bill mentions relevant terms but does not materially affect what users see in their feed.

The scoring system works in two passes. First, it checks whether the bill mentions at least one of: a platform keyword (social media, online platform), an algorithm keyword (algorithm, AI, automated decision), or a privacy keyword (personal data, data broker). Bills that mention none of these are discarded immediately.

Bills that pass the first filter are then scored by accumulating points for positive signals: matching specific phrase patterns like "user control," "opt-out," or "algorithmic transparency" in the title or description; mentioning platforms alongside governance language; combining algorithm terms with transparency or disclosure; referencing youth and social media together; and including enforcement language like civil penalties or attorney general authority. Scores are capped at 10.

This automated scoring is intentionally broad. It catches relevant bills but also picks up noise. The deeper review layer below is designed to correct for that.

Deeper Review

For bills scoring DIRECT, HIGH, or MEDIUM, we fetch the full text of the bill from the legislative source. This text — not just the title and description — is then run through a two-part review process.

The first part is a static analysis: a set of deterministic rules that scan the bill text for specific language patterns and classify the bill against our research taxonomy — what type of content control it creates, what mechanism it uses, and what platforms it covers.

The second part is an AI-assisted review: the bill text is sent to a language model with a structured prompt asking it to answer the research question directly. The model assigns a tier, classifies the bill against the same taxonomy, and provides a short rationale citing specific statutory language. Results are validated against the taxonomy before being stored.

Where LLM review data is available, it is shown alongside the automated score in the bill detail view. The Analytics tab shows how the two scoring systems compare.

Classification Taxonomy

Each reviewed bill is classified along three dimensions:

Content Control Type — what kind of control or restriction the bill creates: User controls, Parental controls, Age-gating, Default settings, Ranking/Recommendation control, Sensitive category suppression, Data Privacy, Opt-out, Opt-in, or Content removal. A bill can have multiple types.

Mechanism — how the bill achieves its effect: Design/defaults (changing how platforms are built or configured), Verification (requiring age or identity checks), Notice & choice (informing users and giving them options), Reporting (transparency reports to government or public), Labeling (warning labels or disclosures in the feed), Notifications in feed (in-app alerts), or Data access (rights to see or download personal data).

Scope — what the bill covers: Social media platforms specifically, General online platforms broadly, or Devices (e.g., parental control requirements on phones rather than apps).

What This Tracker Does Not Do

This tracker does not take a position on any legislation. It does not assess whether bills are good policy, likely to pass, or constitutionally sound. It is a research tool for mapping the legislative landscape around algorithmic feed controls.

The automated scoring system is imperfect. Broad omnibus bills sometimes score highly because they contain relevant language in one small section among dozens of unrelated provisions. The deeper review layer is designed to catch these cases, but human judgment remains essential. Tiers and classifications should be read as research signals, not authoritative legal assessments.

Coverage is limited to legislation that appears in LegiScan's database and is returned by our keyword searches. Bills using unusual terminology or framed outside our keyword set may be missed.
